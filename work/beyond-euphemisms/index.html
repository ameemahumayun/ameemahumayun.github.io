01 — Summary

A product question, not a research question.

Before you can build an LLM screening tool for women in Pakistan, you have to answer something more basic: can the model understand what patients actually say? We found that current models can't do this reliably — and that aggregate accuracy scores hide exactly how and where they fail.

Most healthcare AI is trained on standardized clinical English. In conservative, low-resource contexts, patients communicate through euphemism, metaphor, gesture, and folk vocabulary, and clinicians follow them there to be understood. A model that misreads "shohar sey miltay waqt" (when meeting the husband) doesn't fail loudly — it maps the wrong phrase to the wrong diagnosis and returns a confident answer.

The work paired ethnographic fieldwork in a Lahore OB/GYN ward with a structured evaluation of five LLMs against the language documented there. It produced three things a team can build on: a categorization framework for indirect SRH communication, a failure taxonomy for current models, and a Roman Urdu glossary that fed into Awaaz-e-Sehat, a Gates-funded maternal-health AI effort at LUMS.

Question
Can current LLMs reliably interpret the indirect, culturally coded language used in sexual and reproductive health consultations in Pakistan?
Finding
No — and aggregate accuracy obscures the failures that matter most. The failure modes are systematic and patterned, which means they can be designed against.
Contribution
A two-axis framework for the input space, an evaluation method that surfaces hidden fragility, and a documented glossary of patient language.

02 — Problem framing

Every assumption in healthcare AI breaks in this context.

Pakistan accounts for roughly 4% of global maternal deaths each year — about 27 preventable deaths a day. Each OB/GYN serves around 1,300 patients against the WHO-recommended 1,000, so consultations are short and histories are incomplete. An LLM screening layer could realistically reduce that load, which is why the question is worth taking seriously.

But current healthcare models assume major-language fluency, direct biomedical phrasing, and a single correct vocabulary. None of those hold in a Lahore clinic, where patients move between Urdu, Punjabi, and English; where stigma and low health literacy push communication toward indirection; and where three separate vocabularies coexist for the same anatomical concept, none of them interchangeable.

So the first design decision wasn't about features. It was about establishing ground truth: what does this language actually look like in practice, and where exactly do current models break? Without that, every downstream choice is a guess dressed as a decision.

03 — My role

Co-lead across research, analysis, and evaluation.

I worked across the full arc — research design, fieldwork, synthesis, and model evaluation — as one of two lead researchers, alongside a faculty advisor and a practicing gynecologist who validated clinical interpretation. The work moved through four modes:

Field researcher

Designed the interview protocol, ran patient and clinician interviews and focus groups in Urdu, conducted in-clinic observation, and maintained clinician contact for clarification and new cases.

Analyst & framework author

Led bottom-up coding of communication patterns, built the Domain–Approach categorization framework, and assembled the Roman Urdu corpus that anchored the evaluation.

Model evaluator

Designed the prompt structure and clinical persona, ran five models against the corpus, set up the dual-rater protocol, and coded the qualitative failure modes.

Synthesizer

Connected the linguistic findings to a set of design requirements — what such a system would need to do before it could be deployed responsibly.

04 — Research process

Two stages, designed to feed each other.

Stage 1 documented what indirect SRH communication actually sounds like. Stage 2 tested whether LLMs could interpret it. The output of each was built to become the input of the other — the field corpus became the evaluation prompts, and the model failures pointed back at the patterns that mattered.

A pivot that became a finding

My initial protocol tested three ways of eliciting indirect language: implicit narration, direct questioning, and hypothetical scenarios. All three underperformed, and the scenario approach failed almost immediately. Asked to imagine giving health advice to a friend, one participant explained that her in-laws don't allow her to make friends — the hypothetical collapsed because the social premise didn't exist. I shifted to extended observation of natural clinician–patient interactions and longer expert interviews. The protocol's failure was itself evidence about the conditions these women navigate, and a reminder that the research design has to bend around the population, not the reverse.

All interviews were conducted in Urdu by two female researchers, in a setting where participants already felt comfortable discussing health. Consent, anonymization, and the right to withdraw were handled carefully; one focus group asked not to be recorded at all, and we adapted. Deciding when to push, when to pull back, and when to abandon a method is where research credibility actually comes from.

05 — Key insights

The input space, mapped.

Four patterns inside that framework mattered most for anything built on top of it:

01

Semantic drift inside the local lexicon

Words drift in meaning inside a single language, not just across languages. "Chota operation" ("small operation") means episiotomy in OB/GYN settings but generic minor surgery elsewhere. Borrowed English drifts too: a patient asking "is the child normal?" is asking about delivery mode, not the baby's health.

This is invisible to general-purpose models and clinically consequential — patients routinely list episiotomies as "operations" in pre-op history, inflating surgical records and complicating anesthetic planning.

02

Two kinds of polysemy, with different stakes

Valid polysemy: one descriptor legitimately covers related concepts. "Taanka" (stitch) refers to IUD insertion, sutures, and tubal ligation alike.

Folk polysemy: patients group medically distinct conditions under one word without knowing they differ. "Rasoli" (tumour) gets applied to fibroids, cysts, ectopic pregnancies, and polyps. One word, four diagnoses — and for a screening system, the most dangerous case, because a single confident answer can bury three other possibilities.

03

Three terminology layers run in parallel

Three vocabularies operate at once in the same ecosystem: formal medical Urdu from textbooks (mahbul), formal euphemisms from medicine packaging (andaam nehaani, "hidden part"), and the everyday clinical term (mahwari key raastay, "menstrual pathway"). We asked fifteen OB/GYN doctors about the first two; none recognized them. The textbook word and the working word are functionally unrelated.

04

Gesture is a primary modality, not a fallback

Even when prompted for words, patients returned to pointing. One explained that doctors "are experienced and have been walking along with us," so gesture suffices. Every observed symptom-localization relied on it. Text- and voice-only interfaces structurally exclude this channel — a tool built purely on typed or spoken input discards data the patient is actively trying to provide.

The headline numbers overstate readiness

A model scores "correct" by mapping a polysemous term to one of its valid meanings — without ever signaling that other meanings exist. In screening, each unflagged alternative is a missed clinical pathway. So a 0.82 tells you very little about whether a model is safe to deploy here; it can be confidently wrong in precisely the cases that carry the most risk.

When output broke down entirely

Lower-performing models didn't just answer wrongly; they produced text that wasn't usable as text. Despite explicit Roman Urdu instructions, outputs slipped into Hinglish, Hindi script, and in some cases Telugu, Russian, or garbled Unicode. Around 73% of Pakistani users write digitally in Roman Urdu — script switching makes even accurate content unreadable to the people it's meant for.

Failure taxonomy

Polysemy collapse

"mujhy pishab wali jaga dard hoti hai"

All three top models read this as a urinary infection. In our field data, "urine area" is a common euphemism for the vagina — a vaginal infection was equally likely, and none of the models asked.

Semantic drift

"bacha normal hai?"

Patients use this to ask about delivery mode, not fetal health. No model read it correctly — the surface form contradicts the intent.

Folk belief

athra

No model recognized this as a folk explanation of recurrent miscarriage. Without that frame, a clinician relying on the model would miss the cultural context driving the visit.

Persona slippage

Masculine verb forms in a female persona

Top models occasionally used masculine forms (karunga) despite an explicit female-gynecologist persona. In SRH contexts where provider gender carries weight, this isn't cosmetic.

Register mismatch

Clinically correct, cognitively inaccessible

Models often produced medically accurate explanations unusable for low-literacy patients — describing sterilization as "so that eggs and male sperm cannot meet." Correct, and useless.

The metric was part of the finding

The gap between the two raters became its own result. The gynecologist sometimes scored a response correct where the researcher didn't — not because the model was right, but because she could imagine several clinical pathways the phrase might mean. A real clinician resolves that by asking. The models didn't. Single-rater aggregate accuracy is the wrong measure for this domain, because it rewards confident misreadings. Designing the evaluation to expose that is part of what the work contributes.

07 — Design recommendations

What this context would demand of a system.

The fieldwork and the evaluation together point to three design requirements. These are my recommendations drawn from the evidence — not features that were shipped, but the conditions I'd argue any system has to meet before it can be deployed responsibly here.

Recommendation 01

Treat miscommunication as the default, not the exception.

Most healthcare AI assumes successful communication with error-handling bolted on. The evidence suggests the inverse: assume the patient's surface words diverge from clinical intent, and build verification into the natural flow of conversation. When a patient reports an "operation," the system should clarify which one — framed as gathering information, not doubting the patient. Clarifying questions become a core behavior rather than a fallback.

Recommendation 02

Keep terminology layers parallel, not hierarchical.

The textbook, packaging, and clinical vocabularies serve different functions and can't be collapsed into one "correct" form. A system needs participant-aware selection, function-based mapping, and the ability to move across layers as context shifts. This is more demanding than retrieval-with-a-dictionary — it's a terminology framework a team would extend over time.

Recommendation 03

Design for gesture under privacy constraints.

If gesture is primary, text- and voice-only interfaces are structurally inadequate — but video collides with well-founded fears about visual data in SRH settings. A more viable path is structured body-diagram input: a visual patients can mark, preserving the deictic intent of pointing without the privacy cost. The exact fidelity is a question for prototyping with real users.

An open ethical question I chose to surface, not resolve. Clinicians adapt language to perceived literacy, sometimes withholding the correct word (rasoli, "tumour") because patients hear "cancer." It can improve outcomes, but it's paternalistic, and "low literacy" is routinely conflated with "low health literacy" in ways that bias care. Whether an AI system should replicate this is a genuine design tension, and I think naming it honestly is more useful than pretending there's a clean answer.

08 — Impact

What the work produced.

The research was published at CHI 2026 (Barcelona). Its most concrete real-world thread is the glossary: the documented Roman Urdu corpus and Domain–Approach terminology fed into Awaaz-e-Sehat, a Gates-funded, voice-first maternal-health AI effort at LUMS now associated with Pakistan's first national AI Hub for maternal, newborn, and child health.

A documented input distribution

The corpus and Domain–Approach framework define what indirect patient input actually looks like — usable as ground truth for designing or evaluating any system in this space, rather than guessing at the edge cases.

A reusable failure taxonomy

Polysemy collapse, semantic drift, persona slippage, and script instability are now named, reproducible failure modes — a checklist to test against rather than discover in deployment.

An evaluation method that holds up

The dual-rater protocol — strict to field data, open to plausible clinical alternatives — exposes fragility that single-rater accuracy hides. It transfers to validating new models or prompt strategies before integration.

A glossary in real use

The terminology work feeds into an active maternal-health AI effort — the clearest signal that the documented language is treated as infrastructure, not just a research artifact.

No product screens are shown here because the design recommendations are exactly that — recommendations grounded in the evidence, not a shipped interface. The framework, taxonomy, and glossary are the contribution, and they're the parts that travel beyond this one project.

09 — Reflection

Tradeoffs and limits.

Sample reach. Patients were recruited by convenience sampling within a single Lahore hospital. Expert interviews extended the range — each clinician draws on roughly 1,300 patients — but the demographic skew is real, and Pakistan's linguistic plurality, particularly Pashto- and rural-Punjabi-speaking populations, is underrepresented in the direct patient data.

Positionality. All three researchers are women based in Lahore, fluent in Urdu and Punjabi, with lived experience of local SRH taboos. That enabled interpretive nuance the work depended on. It also means our socioeconomic and educational backgrounds don't fully reflect lower-income participants', especially around health literacy — worth naming rather than smoothing over.

What I'd change. Earlier multi-site sampling across provinces would have surfaced linguistic plurality more cleanly than relying on clinicians' second-hand accounts. I'd also push the gesture-input idea into a low-fidelity prototype inside the same study — a body-diagram interface tested in clinic would have closed the loop from finding to design probe.

The frame worth keeping. The instinct in healthcare AI is to standardize patient language toward what the model can handle. This work argues the opposite: the language is the constraint to design around. Systems that adapt to communities, not communities forced to adapt to systems — a principle that holds anywhere a product meets users on culturally specific ground.
