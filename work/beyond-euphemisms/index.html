01 — The Project

A product question disguised as a research question.

If you're building an LLM-based screening tool for women in Pakistan — where one in 289 births still ends in a maternal death — the first product decision isn't about features. It's about whether the system can interpret what patients actually say in clinic.

Most healthcare AI is trained on standardized clinical English. In conservative, low-resource contexts, patients communicate through euphemism, metaphor, gesture, and folk vocabulary — and clinicians who want to be understood follow them there. A model that can't parse "shohar sey miltay waqt" (when meeting the husband) doesn't fail gracefully; it fails silently, mapping the wrong word to the wrong diagnosis.

This project paired ethnographic fieldwork in a Lahore OB/GYN ward with a structured evaluation of five LLMs against the language we documented there. The output: a categorization framework for indirect SRH communication, a failure taxonomy for current models, and a set of design principles that became input to a real product roadmap.

The Question
Can current LLMs reliably interpret the indirect, culturally-coded language patients use in sexual and reproductive health consultations?
The Answer
Not reliably — and aggregate accuracy hides the worst of it. The failure modes are systematic and patterned, which means they can be designed against.
The Contribution
A two-axis framework for the input space, an evaluation methodology that surfaces hidden fragility, and three architectural principles for healthcare AI in culturally complex contexts.
Where It Landed
Findings informed the design direction of a maternal-health AI initiative for Pakistan. The product implementation remains under NDA.
02 — Problem Framing

Health language is taboo language.

Pakistan accounts for roughly 4% of global maternal deaths each year — about 27 preventable deaths per day. The clinical workforce is overstretched: each OB/GYN in Pakistan serves around 1,300 patients against the WHO-recommended 1:1,000. Short consultation times produce incomplete histories and missed warning signs. An LLM-based screening layer could meaningfully reduce that load.

But every assumption baked into current healthcare LLMs cuts against this context. They assume English or major-language fluency. They assume direct, biomedical phrasing. They assume one "correct" vocabulary. None of those hold in a Lahore clinic, where patients move between Urdu, Punjabi, and English; where stigma and low health literacy push communication toward indirection; and where three different vocabularies coexist for the same anatomical concept without any being interchangeable.

Phrases like "missed my date" or "bring a visitor" are widespread in SRH discourse but hard for AI to parse. SRH communication depends on exactly the forms AI struggles to interpret. — from the paper

Before any feature could be designed, we needed a grounded answer to a more basic product question: what does this language actually look like in practice, and where do current models break? Without that, every downstream design choice is a guess.

Cross-functional collaboration. The project sat between clinical practice, NLP/ML, and product. Methodological pivot

My initial protocol tested three elicitation strategies — implicit narration, direct questioning, and hypothetical scenarios. All three underperformed. The scenario-based approach failed almost immediately: when asked to imagine giving health advice to a friend, one participant explained that her in-laws don't allow her to make friends. Social isolation was so structural that the hypothetical collapsed. I pivoted to extended observation of natural clinician–patient interactions and longer expert interviews. The protocol's failure was itself a finding about the conditions women navigate — and a reminder that the research scaffold has to bend around the population, not the other way around.

All interviews were conducted in Urdu by two female researchers in a setting where participants already felt comfortable discussing health matters. Consent, anonymization, and the right to withdraw were handled with extra care; one focus group requested no recording at all, and we adapted accordingly. This kind of judgment work — when to push, when to pull back, when to abandon a tool entirely — is where research credibility actually comes from.

05 — Key Insights

The input space, mapped.

Bottom-up coding produced a framework with two axes. Referential domain describes what is being talked about. Communicative approach describes how. Together they define the full input space a system in this context has to handle.

Inside that framework, four patterns mattered most for product design:

01

Semantic drift inside the local lexicon

Words don't just translate poorly across languages — they drift inside a language. "Chota operation" ("small operation") means episiotomy in OB/GYN contexts but is a generic term for minor surgery elsewhere. Borrowed English words drift too: when a patient asks "is the child normal?", she's asking whether the delivery will be vaginal, not whether the baby is healthy.

This is invisible to general-purpose LLMs and clinically consequential. Patients routinely list episiotomies as "operations" during preoperative history-taking, inflating reported surgical history and complicating anesthetic planning.

02

Polysemy comes in two flavors

Valid polysemy: one general descriptor legitimately covers several related concepts. "Taanka" (stitch) is used for IUD insertion, surgical sutures, and tubal ligation.

Folk polysemy: patients incorrectly group medically distinct phenomena under one term. "Rasoli" (tumour) is applied indiscriminately to fibroids, cysts, ectopic pregnancies, and polyps. The same word can mean four different diagnoses, and patients don't know they're different. For a screening system, this is the most dangerous failure mode — a single response can mask three other possibilities.

03

Three terminology layers coexist simultaneously

Three vocabularies operate in parallel in the same ecosystem: formal medical Urdu in textbooks (mahbul), formal euphemisms on medical packaging (andaam nehaani, "hidden part"), and the everyday clinical term (mahwari key raastay, "menstrual pathway"). When we asked fifteen OB/GYN doctors about the first two, none recognized them. The textbook word and the everyday word are functionally unrelated.

04

Gesture is a primary modality, not a fallback

Even when explicitly prompted for verbal alternatives, patients consistently returned to pointing. One participant explained that doctors "are experienced and have been walking along with us," so gesture is enough. Every observed symptom-localization interaction relied on it. Text- and voice-only interfaces structurally exclude this mode, which means any screening tool depending purely on typed or spoken input loses data the patient is actively trying to give.

Why the headline numbers overstate performance

Even high-scoring models were fragile in specific, predictable ways. A model can score "correct" by mapping a polysemous term to one of its valid meanings — and never signal that other meanings exist. In a screening context, each unflagged alternative is a missed clinical pathway. The headline number tells you almost nothing about deployment readiness.

When the output broke down entirely

Lower-performing models didn't just produce wrong answers; they produced text that wasn't usable as text. Despite explicit Roman Urdu instructions, outputs dropped into Hinglish, Hindi script, and in some cases Telugu, Russian, or garbled Unicode. Roman Urdu is how 73% of Pakistani users write digitally — script switching makes otherwise accurate output unreadable to the population it's meant to serve.

Failure taxonomy

Polysemy Collapse

"mujhy pishab wali jaga dard hoti hai"

All three top models read this as urinary infection. In our field data, "urine area" is a common euphemism for the vagina — a vaginal infection was equally likely. None of the models asked.

Semantic Drift

"bacha normal hai?"

Patients use this to ask about delivery mode (vaginal vs. C-section), not fetal health. No model interpreted it correctly. The surface form contradicts the communicative intent.

Folk Belief

athra

No model recognized this as a folk explanation of recurrent miscarriage. Without that frame, a clinician using the model would miss the cultural context driving the visit.

Persona Slippage

Masculine verb forms in female-persona output

All top models occasionally used masculine forms (karunga) despite an explicit female-gynecologist persona. In SRH contexts where provider gender carries weight, this isn't cosmetic.

Register Mismatch

Clinically correct, cognitively inaccessible

Models frequently produced explanations that were medically accurate but unusable for low-literacy patients — describing sterilization as "so that eggs and male sperm cannot meet." Correct, unusable.

Why dual-rater evaluation matters

The disparity between researcher and expert scoring became a finding of its own. The gynecologist sometimes scored a response correct when the researcher did not — not because the model was right, but because she could imagine multiple clinical pathways the phrase could have meant. A real clinician disambiguates by asking. The models did not. Single-rater aggregate accuracy is the wrong metric for this domain; it credits confident misreadings. The evaluation framework itself is part of what this project contributes.

07 — Design Implications

What this context demands of an AI product.

The qualitative findings and the evaluation together produced three architectural principles. None are about polish — they're about what the system has to be able to do before it can be deployed responsibly.

Principle 01

Treat miscommunication as the default state, not the exception.

Most healthcare AI assumes successful communication with error-handling on top. Our findings suggest the inverse: the system should assume the patient's surface words diverge from their clinical intent, and embed verification into the natural flow of conversation. When a patient lists an "operation," the system clarifies which procedure — without making the patient feel doubted. Verification becomes information gathering rather than error correction. In product terms: clarifying questions are a feature, not a fallback.

Principle 02

Maintain terminology layers in parallel, not in hierarchy.

The three coexisting vocabularies — textbook, packaging, clinical — serve different communicative functions and can't be collapsed into one "correct" form. Systems need participant-aware selection, function-based mapping, and bidirectional translation that can move across layers as conversation context shifts. This is more demanding than retrieval-with-a-dictionary; it's a strategic terminology framework the product team can extend over time.

Principle 03

Design for gesture under privacy constraints.

If gesture is a primary modality, text- and voice-only interfaces are structurally inadequate. The obvious solution — video — collides with strong, well-founded concerns about visual data collection in SRH contexts. A more viable path is structured body-diagram input: visual representations patients can mark, preserving the deictic intent of pointing without the privacy cost of video. This raises further design questions that should be resolved through prototyping with real users.

An open ethical design question. Clinicians adapt their communication based on perceived patient literacy, sometimes withholding the technically correct word (rasoli meaning "tumour") because patients hear it as "cancer." This produces better outcomes — but it's also paternalistic, and "low literacy" is routinely conflated with "low health literacy" in ways that bias care. Whether and how an AI system should replicate this is a design question the team will keep returning to. We surfaced it deliberately rather than resolving it prematurely.

08 — Translation to Product

From research framework to product direction.

This research predates and shaped the design direction of a maternal-health AI initiative for Pakistan — particularly around how the system handles patient input, how clinical risk-flagging is structured, and which disambiguation behaviors are required before any output reaches a clinician.

The specific contributions to the product roadmap:

A ground-truth input distribution

The Roman Urdu corpus and Domain–Approach categorization defined what indirect inputs actually look like — not as edge cases, but as the primary input distribution the system has to handle on day one.

A failure taxonomy informing guardrails

Polysemy collapse, semantic drift, persona slippage, and script instability became specific behaviors the product team could build guardrails against, rather than discovering in deployment.

An evaluation methodology that holds up

The dual-rater protocol — strict to field data, open to plausible clinical alternatives — exposes hidden fragility. This now shapes how new model versions and prompt strategies are validated before integration.

Design constraints, not just findings

The three architectural principles became working constraints for the conversational and multimodal design — particularly around verification flows, terminology handling, and the gesture-input problem.

09 — Reflection

Tradeoffs and limits.

Sample reach. Patients were recruited through convenience sampling within a single charitable hospital in Lahore. We supplemented with expert interviews — clinicians in Pakistan each serve roughly 1,300 patients, giving them broad experiential range — but the demographic skew is real. Pakistan's linguistic plurality, particularly Pashto-speaking and rural-Punjabi-speaking populations, is underrepresented in our direct patient data.

Positionality. All three researchers are women based in Lahore, fluent in Urdu and Punjabi, with lived experience of local taboos around SRH. This enabled interpretive nuance the work depended on. It also means our privileged socioeconomic and educational backgrounds don't fully reflect the experiences of lower-income participants, particularly around health literacy. Naming this matters more than papering over it.

What I'd do differently. Earlier multi-site sampling across provinces would have surfaced linguistic plurality more cleanly than relying on clinicians' second-hand accounts. And I'd push the gestural-input design question into a low-fidelity prototype within the same study — a body-diagram interface tested in clinic could have closed the loop from research finding to formative design probe.

The frame I want to keep. The instinct in healthcare AI is to standardize patient language toward something the model can handle. This work argues the inverse: the language is the constraint the system has to be designed around. AI that adapts to communities, not communities that adapt to AI. That principle is portable beyond this domain — it applies anywhere a product meets users on culturally specific terrain.
